Data Story

The hidden cost of a bigger screen.

Australian TV buyers compare brand, size, and picture quality first. This story makes the case for adding one more thing to the list — energy.

Story Overview

A television isn't a one-time purchase. It keeps costing money every time it's switched on.

This data story is written for Australian households choosing a TV. The goal is simple — make energy consumption feel practical by connecting screen technology, active power, star ratings, and estimated annual cost into a single decision.

Audience

Australian households

People comparing televisions for everyday use, especially buyers who want a larger screen without being surprised by long-term electricity costs.

Interest

Lower running costs

The visualisation helps viewers translate watts, screen size, and star ratings into a practical shopping question.

Decision

Choose efficiently

The story encourages consumers to weigh active power and energy labels alongside price when making the final pick.

The Story

Bigger screens can be exciting — but efficiency decides the long-term value.

1

Start with technology. Plasma models sit far above the rest for active power, while LCD and LED options generally use less electricity for the same viewing experience.

2

Then consider size. A larger screen usually draws more power. Over years of daily viewing, that gap becomes a noticeable household cost.

3

Finish with the label. Star ratings should be read alongside watts and expected daily usage — not in isolation.

Technology shapes efficiency

The technology inside the screen strongly affects how much electricity the TV uses during regular viewing.

The cost correlation

As active power usage increases, so does the estimated annual electricity cost — almost in a straight line.

Key takeaways

  • LCD and LED technology remain strong efficiency choices.
  • Higher wattage translates almost directly to a higher annual electricity bill.
  • Large screens aren't automatically bad — but their running costs deserve a check.
  • Star ratings provide a quick proxy for long-term efficiency.

The best TV isn't only the biggest or brightest.

Before buying, compare the energy label and active power rating with the same care you give to screen size and price.
